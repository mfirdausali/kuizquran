Why the wrong answers matter more than the right one

Every distractor is a lesson
in disguise.

A multiple-choice question is only as good as its traps. Each word carries five hand-engineered distractors, blending four kinds of "almost-right" — so a wrong tap teaches you something true about the word.

Semantic

A word that means almost the same thing — so you learn the exact revealed word, not a synonym that would "do."

Visual

A word that looks alike in the script — including case-ending and tanwin traps that quietly teach you Arabic grammar (i'rāb).

Phonetic

A word that sounds alike when recited — targeting the exact slips the tongue makes in salah.

Contextual

A real word from elsewhere in the same surah, in the wrong place — so you learn word order, not just vocabulary.

The engine · "QuranFSRS-Implicit"

Monoxer's discipline.
FSRS's clock. No self-grading.

The forced-choice card is the memory test. Correctness plus how fast you answered deterministically produce the review grade — so you can never lie to yourself about your hifz, and there's zero Anki-style friction.

Engineered from a smartphone-addict's playbook — bounded by adab.

We use the same loops that keep people on TikTok for hours — variable reward, near-miss tension, cliffhangers, progress bars — so heavy screen time becomes ibadah instead of waste. But because this is the Quran, hard rails keep it beneficial, never exploitative:

  • Satiety cap — it refuses to let you cram. Extra reps before a card is due don't help retention anyway.
  • No dark patterns — no "your streak DIES in 1 hour." Reminders are dignified, tied to your salah times.
  • Streak-idolatry brake — once streaks get long, the number shrinks and "ayahs retained" takes over.
  • Retention honesty — a decaying word is always surfaced. We never fake mastery to boost engagement.
  • Meaning, never parroting — every word links to its meaning and its place in the story.
  • Graceful lapses — sabr jamīl: a miss damps your progress, it never zeroes you back to the start.
